Victoria becomes the first Australian state to sign a treaty with its Indigenous population:
"Historic moment for our people"

Photo used for educational purposes only. ® Vrst nws

For the first time in Australian history, a state has entered into a formal treaty with its Indigenous people. In Victoria, where Melbourne is located, Aboriginal people will now have a say in policies that affect their lives. The state government calls it a historic step toward reconciliation and recognition of the injustices inflicted on Indigenous people for centuries.

After two days of debate, the Upper House of the Victorian Parliament passed the Treaty Bill by 21 votes to 16.

"This treaty gives the Aboriginal community the power to shape the policies that affect their lives," says State Premier Jacinta Allan. "This is how we build a fairer and stronger Victoria for everyone."

The local agreement comes two years after the national referendum on an Indigenous voice in the constitution failed. That referendum was about a constitutional amendment to recognize Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples as Australia's original inhabitants. Nearly 60 percent of the Australian population opposed the proposal at the time.

"

The treaty marks the beginning of a new era

Ngarra Murray, co-chair of the First Peoples' Assembly

Ngarra Murray, co-chair of the First Peoples' Assembly, therefore calls the treaty "a historic moment for our people." "We will tell our children about this day, and they will tell their children, and pass on to future generations the story of how decades of Aboriginal resilience and activism led to Australia's first treaty."

After the proposal was rejected at the national level, Indigenous people continued to campaign for greater recognition of their initiatives. It took over 10 years to finally implement the treaty in Victoria.

"The treaty marks the beginning of a new era, one in which the 60,000 years of knowledge and culture of First Peoples are respected and celebrated. It is an opportunity for all Victorians to acknowledge our shared history, heal, and move forward together," Murray said.

Inequality in Australia:

The gap between Indigenous people and the rest of Australia is deep and has grown historically. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people make up approximately 3.8 percent of the population, but fare worse than other Australians in almost every way. They live an average of nine years less, commit suicide twice as often, and are significantly overrepresented in prisons.

This inequality has its roots in Australia's violent colonial past. When the country was incorporated as a penal colony by the British Crown in 1788, the Indigenous population was expelled, poisoned, and murdered. The "White Australia" policy, which systematically removed Indigenous children from their parents, persisted until the 1970s.

For decades, the atrocities and traumas of colonization were ignored or concealed. That history still leaves deep scars: many Aboriginal people still struggle with marginalization, poverty, and racism. The new treaty should help finally close that gap.

What exactly does the treaty entail?

Part of the treaty is the continuation of the work of the Yoorrook Commission, which has been engaged in fact-finding in recent years. This commission, whose name refers to the word "truth" in the Wemba Wemba language, states, among other things, that during the colonization of Victoria, over three-quarters of the Indigenous population was killed in the 20 years. The commission therefore concludes irrefutably that genocide was committed against the Indigenous population.

In addition, the treaty establishes a new democratic body. This body, to be called the Gellung Warl Commission, will henceforth be consulted on laws and policies affecting Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

In this way, the commission will ensure that the government meets its obligations to close the gap between Indigenous and white Australians.

One concrete consequence of the treaty agreement is a revision of the school curriculum. The updated curriculum should offer children insight into the dark chapters of Indigenous Australian history. The Victorian state government will also formally apologize to the country's Indigenous people.

What remains unclear, however, is the price tag for implementing the treaty. It's estimated that the new initiatives will likely cost tens of millions of dollars annually.

The local opposition is already strongly opposed to the agreement and has promised to immediately scrap the proposal if it comes to power. The new state elections at the end of 2026 could therefore once again sweep the reforms aside.

Pioneer Victoria

With this step, Victoria becomes the first Australian state to adopt Voice, Treaty, and Truth. These are the three pillars of reform outlined in the 2017 Uluru Statement from the Heart, a groundbreaking document drafted by Indigenous leaders calling for legal recognition.

"

We can show the rest of Australia that this isn't something to be afraid of. In fact, it will bring us closer together.

Jill Gallagher, Chief Executive of Victoria's Local Health Board

Jill Gallagher, chief executive of Victoria's Aboriginal Health Board, told the ABC public broadcaster she hopes this will be an example for other parts of the country.

"We can show the rest of Australia that this is nothing to be afraid of. In fact, it will bring us closer together."

The reconciliation process appears to be making significant progress. A growing number of states are actively pursuing similar initiatives. For example, a commission has been established in South Australia to represent the voice of the local Indigenous population.
